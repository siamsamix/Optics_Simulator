The Fraunhofer (Far-Field) Regime

**Fraunhofer diffraction** describes light propagation in the *far-field*, where the distance $z$ between the aperture and the observation screen is so large that incoming and outgoing waves can be treated as planar. Under this condition, the phase variation across the aperture is linear, and the light pattern on the screen becomes the **Fourier Transform** of the aperture transmittance function:

I(x', y') ∝ | ∬ U(x, y) e-i 2π (x x' + y y') / (λ z) dx dy |²

The physical approximation is valid when the screen distance $z$ exceeds the **Fraunhofer Distance** (or Rayleigh criterion) $d_F = \frac{2 D^2}{\lambda}$, where $D$ is the maximum dimension of the aperture and $\lambda$ is the wavelength.

Key Far-Field Phenomena to Explore:

  • **The Fourier Reciprocity**: Make the aperture size (e.g. Slit Width or Circular Radius) smaller. You will notice that the diffraction pattern on the screen gets *larger*! This reciprocal relationship is a fundamental property of the Fourier Transform (short signals spread out in frequency).
  • **Single Slit Envelopes**: Select the **Double Slit** or **N-Slit Grating** presets. The overall intensity is bound by a broad envelope dashed curve. This envelope is mathematically identical to the single slit diffraction pattern of a single slit width $w$! The high-frequency spikes underneath are due to multi-source interference.
  • **Diffraction Grating Peaks**: Choose **N-Slit Grating** and increase the number of slits. The peaks become extremely sharp and bright. This narrowing of peaks as $N_s$ increases is why diffraction gratings are used in spectrometers to separate wavelengths with high resolution. The peaks satisfy the grating equation: $d \sin\theta = m \lambda$, visible as periodic bright bands.
  • **Airy Disk (Circular Aperture)**: Select the circular aperture. The concentric rings surrounding the bright central peak are the Airy Disk. The first dark ring occurs at angle $\theta = 1.22 \frac{\lambda}{2R}$, setting the ultimate resolution limit of lenses, microscopes, and telescopes.
