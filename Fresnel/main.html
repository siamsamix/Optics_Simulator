The Fresnel Regime

Unlike **Fraunhofer diffraction** (which describes light in the *far-field* where rays are treated as parallel), **Fresnel diffraction** calculates the light field in the *near-field*. In this regime, the curvature of the wavefront cannot be neglected, and the light pattern is highly sensitive to the distance $z$ from the aperture.

The physical regime is characterized by the **Fresnel Number** $F = \frac{a^2}{\lambda z}$, where $a$ is the characteristic aperture size, $\lambda$ is the wavelength, and $z$ is the propagation distance:

  • $F \gg 1$: **Geometric Optics Regime**. The pattern is a sharp shadow of the aperture, with tiny fringes at the boundaries.
  • $F \approx 1$: **Fresnel Near-Field**. Strong interference effects occur. You see extreme "ringing" at slit boundaries, constructive/destructive centers, and focusing effects.
  • $F \ll 1$: **Fraunhofer Far-Field**. The pattern becomes the Fourier Transform of the aperture. The geometric shape is completely lost, and only angular lobes remain.

Try this interesting physics experiment:

Choose the **Circular Aperture** preset and set the distance $z$ to around 2.0 cm. Look closely at the center of the diffraction pattern. You will see a small, bright peak at the absolute center of the dark shadow. This is the famous **Poisson's Spot** (or Arago Spot). It occurs because spherical waves diffracting from the circular boundary travel exactly equal distances to the central axis, resulting in constructive interference!

Now, switch to the **Zone Plate** preset. Set the distance $z$ to match the **Design Focal Length** shown in the parameters (e.g. 15 cm). You will observe a massive, sharp intensity peak at the center. This is because the zone plate blocks half-period zones that would cause destructive interference, acting as a focusing lens via diffraction alone!
